Zelkea moves money between two kinds of accounts, using one kind of address.

That's really it. Everything else in these docs is detail on top of that one idea.

## The three things you need to know

  </Card>

  <Card title="Businesses" icon="store" href="/businesses">

## How a payment actually moves

Say `malick@mpesa` wants to pay `netflix@us` \$14.99.

1. Netflix (a **business**) calls `POST /payments` with Malick's handle, the amount, and a one-time code Malick gave them.
2. Zelkea looks up which **institution** issued `malick@mpesa` — in this case, M-Pesa.
3. Zelkea instructs a same-day settlement between M-Pesa's balance and Netflix's balance.
4. Both sides get a webhook the moment it lands. No polling, no waiting.

No card numbers changed hands. No bank details were typed anywhere. Netflix never even needed to know M-Pesa exists as a company — the handle was the only thing that mattered.

## Two ways money moves

<Columns cols={2}>
  <Card title="Transfers" href="/transfers">
    Handle-to-handle. One sender, one receiver. Used for P2P, remittances, and institution-to-institution settlement.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Payments" href="/payments">
    Customer-to-business. Authorized with a one-time code. Can split a single payment across multiple recipients.
  </Card>
</Columns>

<Tip>
  If money is moving **between two people or two institutions**, you want Transfers. If a **business is getting paid** by a customer, you want Payments. That's the only decision you need to make.
</Tip>

## What Zelkea doesn't do

This matters as much as what it does do:

- **Zelkea doesn't hold a ledger for you.** Your institution's balance is your source of truth — Zelkea just moves money and tells you when it happened.
- **Zelkea doesn't reverse transactions.** Transfers settle instantly and are final. If money needs to come back, that's a new transfer, initiated by whoever's responsible for that relationship — same as a real bank wire.
- **Zelkea doesn't see or store sensitive banking details.** A handle never contains an account number, card number, or wallet ID. That information stays inside the issuing institution.
