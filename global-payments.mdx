Cross-border payments have traditionally meant one of two things: a correspondent banking relationship (slow, expensive, requires pre-funded accounts in every corridor), or a patchwork of local integrations, one per country you actually need to reach. Zelkea replaces both with the same call you'd use to send money domestically.

## A transfer doesn't care which country it's going to

There's no separate "international transfer" endpoint. `sender` and `receiver` are just handles — if the institutions behind them happen to be in different countries, nothing about the request changes:

```bash Send from Dakar to Tokyo
curl --request POST 'https://api.zelkea.com/transfers' \
  --header "Authorization: Bearer $ZELKEA_API_KEY" \
  --header 'idempotency-key: 1e4f5a8b-6c9d-8e7f-6a5b-4c7b9e4a3c2d' \
  --header 'Content-Type: application/json' \
  --data-raw '{
    "sender": "tom@ecobank",
    "receiver": "restaurant1@jp",
    "amount": 40,
    "currency": "USD"
  }'
```

```json Response
{
  "id": "tx_f9k2m7",
  "sender": "tom@ecobank",
  "receiver": "restaurant1@jp",
  "amount": 40,
  "currency": "USD",
  "status": "completed",
  "created_at": "2026-07-10T14:02:33Z",
  "reference": "dinner-tokyo-trip"
}
```

Tom never opened a Japanese bank account, and the restaurant never needed to know what Ecobank is. The handle is the only thing that had to be correct.

## Why this doesn't need a correspondent bank

Traditional cross-border transfers rely on a chain of correspondent banks, each taking a cut and adding a day of settlement delay — sometimes several. Zelkea settles directly between the two institutions' balances on the network, in one hop, regardless of how many countries sit between them geographically.

## Common use cases

- **A traveler paying a local merchant abroad** — no currency exchange counter, no foreign transaction fee tacked on by a card network.
- **A worker sending remittances home** — instant settlement instead of a multi-day wait, at a fraction of what traditional remittance corridors charge.
- **A business paying an overseas supplier** — one transfer call, regardless of which country the supplier's bank is in.
